# Headcar, AI Meetup talk (Berlin · Tue Jun 30, 20:25–20:35)

A **10-minute, demo-forward, inspirational** talk for an AI/tech crowd, then **open demo +
mingling until 21:30**. The real subject is **how I went from not building anything to a
brain-controlled car in ~2 months**, the car is the *proof*, not the point. Honest,
personal, a little funny. (Deep car/technical content lives in `PRESENTATION_SCRIPT.md` for Q&A.)

**The spine:** *Iron Man taught me to build your way out of any hole → meeting Tejas Kumar
showed me we live in a builder's era → so I vibe-coded a brain-controlled car without really
knowing how to code → you can start too, but the tools got free, taste didn't.*

**Why it lands with an AI crowd:** the honest **"I vibe-coded it and I still can't fully
code"** angle is exactly this moment in tech, and the **"AI lowered the barrier to *starting*,
not the bar for doing it well"** turn keeps it tasteful instead of hype. (Set up in the hook,
paid off in the close.)

> Personal facts used: started ~2 months ago; vibe-codes (learning real code on the side via a
> calculator app); inspired by Iron Man + meeting Tejas Kumar at church in Berlin; earlier uni
> project = a miniature traffic light. **Confirm:** the `Tejas` code-folder line only works if you
> actually named the folder after him, cut it if that's a coincidence.

## 1 · Hook (0:45)
*(car in hand, no remote, let them see there's no controller)*
> "In a few minutes, I'm going to drive this car across the room, with my face. *(pause, hold
> it up)* No remote, no app. And here's the part I'm weirdly proud of: I built it in the last two months, 
> and I **still can't really code.** I vibe-coded the whole thing. *(beat)* So this is a
> talk about what that makes possible, and the one thing AI still couldn't hand me."
> *(pause)*

## 2 · The kid who loved Iron Man (1:30)
> "I grew up obsessed with Iron Man, not the flying or the fighting, but the way Tony Stark
> could always **build his way out of any hole.** Stuck in a cave? Build a suit. Dying? Invent
> a new element. That idea, that you can engineer your way out of *anything*, stuck with me.
>
> But for years it stayed a fantasy. I *used* technology; but I'd never built it before. I assumed building
> the real stuff was for people who actually knew how to code."

## 3 · The spark, the builder's era (1:45) 
> "Quick show of hands, who here has made *anything* with AI this year? A script, a little app,
> a website, anything you couldn't have a couple years ago. *(react to the room, see below)*
>
> **Reacting to the hands (works either way):**
> - *Lots of hands:* "Yeah, look around the room. That's not normal. That's new."
> - *Barely any:* "Almost none? Perfect, because two months ago, my hand wouldn't have gone up either."
>
> Either way, this shift of people now having the tools to build has a name, and I got it from **Tejas Kumar**, I met him at church here
> in Berlin. We started out just friends, but when he heard I study Mechatronics and saw a few of
> my uni projects, he put words to it: we live in a **builder's era.** With AI, the gap between an
> idea you *care* about and a working version of it has basically collapsed. You don't need ten
> years of computer science first, you need to start, and keep going when it breaks. *(He's been
> enough of a push that, fair warning, my code literally lives in a folder with his name on it.)*
>
> So two months ago, I started."


## 4 · Starting honest, I vibe-code (1:45)
> "Here's the honest part. I don't fully know how to read or write code yet. What I do is
> **vibe-coding**, I describe what I want to an AI, it writes the code, I run it, it breaks, we
> fix it, repeat. Some people sneer at that. I think it's the most exciting on-ramp to building
> there has ever been.
>
> But I noticed something fast: the AI will build you *whatever* you ask for, including the
> wrong thing, beautifully. It never once told me what was actually worth building. **That part,
> the taste, stayed on me.** *(seed, pays off in the close.)*
>
> And I'm not staying at vibe-coding either, I'm learning the real fundamentals on the side.
> Right now I'm slowly building a calculator app *by hand* to actually understand the code.
> Vibe-coding got me in the door; now I'm learning the house."

## 5 · The audacious build, headcar (1:30)
> "At uni I'd built the *assigned* things, a little traffic light that flips on a timer, a car with a camera that stayed between 2 lines,
> but that was homework, someone else's idea. Headcar was the first thing I built because I *wanted*
> it to exist. And two months in, a little too ambitious, I went straight for a car you control
> with your brain. A headband reads the signals from my face and head, my laptop figures out the
> gesture, and it drives the car.
>
> And it was a **comedy of problems**, four counterfeit motor chips I had to diagnose one pin at
> a time, the textbooks being flat wrong about my own hardware, signals that looked like pure
> noise. Vibe-coding didn't make the problems vanish, but it meant I could keep building my way
> out of every hole. Very Tony Stark."

## 6 · LIVE DEMO (2:00), *the payoff*
> "Enough talking, let me show you."
*(narrate each action; hands off the keyboard, look at the room; see the demo run-sheet below)*
1. **Calibrate**, "Five seconds sitting still… four green dots."
2. **Arm**, "Until now it ignored me completely, safety layer one."
3. **Forward**, "Harder clench, more speed." *(light → hard)*
4. **Reverse**, "Eyebrows up." 5. **Steer**, "Tilt to turn."
6. **Stop**, "I disarm, and even if my laptop died, a half-second watchdog stops it on its own."

## 7 · Takeaway + close (1:00), *the payoff*
> "Right at the start, I said there was one thing AI still couldn't hand me. Here it is, and I
> hinted at it earlier: it lowered the barrier to *starting*; it never lowered the bar for doing
> something *well*. The tools got free; **taste didn't.** And that's the *exciting* part,
> because taste, actually *caring* about the thing, is something you already have. The AI can
> write the code. It can't *want* the thing for you.
>
> Two months ago I assumed I needed permission, or a degree, or to be a 'real' programmer first.
> I didn't, I needed an idea I actually cared about, an AI, and a bit of Tony Stark stubbornness
> when it broke. You've already got the part that matters. *(beat)* So go build the thing you care
> about this week, start before you're ready, and come drive the car after. Thank you."

## Live-demo plan for the room (your "not sure yet")

**Biggest demo-saver: use your phone hotspot, not the venue WiFi.** Meetup WiFi is unreliable
and often blocks device-to-device traffic (AP isolation), your `Jon iPhone 16` hotspot is
portable and known-good. Connect the car at `ws://172.20.10.2:81` (raw IP, `car.local` often
won't resolve on Windows).

**Pre-talk setup (do it before you're on stage):**
- [ ] Car powered on a **full pack**; laptop on the hotspot; dashboard open in Chrome.
- [ ] Connected + **calibrated to 4 green dots** while sitting still. Keep it **disarmed** until you're ready.
- [ ] A small **clear patch of floor** (~2–3 m). No floor? Keep the on-stage drive tiny; save free-roaming for the open demo.

**Keep the on-stage drive SHORT and reliable:** forward (show the intensity ramp) + one turn +
the watchdog stop. That's the wow, don't push your luck on stage.

**Backup (so a flaky contact dot never becomes dead air):**
- Have a **recorded run queued**, either a screen-recording of a clean drive, or load
  `laptop/recordings/headcar-2026-06-19_08-41-45.json` into the dashboard's replay.
- If contact is bad: narrate the fallback ("here's the same thing from a recorded session") and move on.

**Open demo time (20:35–21:30):**
- Let a few volunteers wear the Muse and try, but **calibration is per-person** (~5 s, and hair/
  contact varies), so expect some misfires. Keep *your* calibrated profile as the reliable showcase.
- ⚠️ **Battery:** one pack runs ~11 min *continuous*. An hour of on-and-off demoing will outlast a
  single charge, **bring the USB charger and/or a second 18650 pack** and top up between drives.

## Rehearsal plan (built from how great meetup/demo talks land)

**Principles to rehearse *into* the talk** (sources below):
- **Cut to 1–2 things.** The tech is the *least* important part, story + passion win. Your two: the *artifact-vs-intent reframe* and the *live drive*. Everything else is cuttable.
- **Hook in 20–30 s, spoken not written.** Start with the car in hand, no remote, then **pause** after the first line (the pause kills the "reading a script" feel).
- **WOW early, don't show everything.** Spend your time on the strongest thing (the drive). Don't tour every feature.
- **Live-demo discipline:** hands *off* the keyboard while you talk, look at the room, pause at key beats. **45-second rule**, if the demo breaks, drop to the backup and move on; never troubleshoot live.
- **Drill the hook separately** until automatic, it's the highest-stakes 30 seconds and usually under-rehearsed.
- **Spaced repetition** beats one marathon: short daily reps, then full timed run-throughs, then a live human audience.

**4-day schedule (Fri Jun 26 → Tue Jun 30):**
- **Fri, chunk + hook (~15 min).** Read the whole script aloud once for feel. Then drill **Beat 1 (hook)** and **Beat 2 (reframe)** ~5× each. No timing yet.
- **Sat, demo on real gear (~25 min).** Set up *exactly* like the venue (phone hotspot, laptop, car). Run calibrate → arm → forward → reverse → steer → stop **3–4×** until it's muscle memory. Once, deliberately "fail" and practice switching to the recorded `.json` replay in <45 s.
- **Sun, first full timed run (~30 min).** Whole talk, out loud, with the car, **timed**. Target the **8:30–9:15** sweet spot. The script's budgeted ~9:00, if you come in under ~8, you're rushing; slow the pauses and let the demo breathe rather than adding words. Do it twice.
- **Mon, human audience + polish (~30 min).** Full run in front of a friend (or phone-record and watch back) for clarity + pace feedback. Re-drill the hook once. **Lock the narrative.**
- **Tue (talk day), light touch.** One calm run-through in the morning (don't over-rehearse day-of). At the venue: arrive early, hotspot up, calibrate to 4 green dots, queue the backup replay, charger/spare pack ready. Deliver.

**Sources:** [Wiredcraft, 7 tips for meetup talks](https://wiredcraft.com/blog/successful-meetup-talks-tips/) · [GitLab, what makes a great tech talk](https://about.gitlab.com/blog/what-makes-a-great-tech-talk/) · [Scott Berkun, how to give a perfect demo](https://scottberkun.com/2011/how-to-give-a-perfect-demo/) · [Presales Collective, first 5 minutes of a demo](https://www.presalescollective.com/content/how-to-nail-the-first-5-minutes-of-your-product-demo-and-why-it-matters) · [Moxie, opening a speech, first 60 s](https://www.moxieinstitute.com/how-to-open-a-speech-60-seconds/)
